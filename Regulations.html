General Guidance on the Bank's Regulations

The principal role of the Bank is to promote monetary and financial stability that is conducive to the sustainable growth of the Malaysian economy.

In doing so, the Bank is entrusted with several functions including the fostering of a sound, progressive and inclusive financial system. Accordingly, the Bank’s regulations are based on robust principles and are designed to achieve parity, proportionality and neutrality (more about principles)​

Scope of regulated entities and activities

Proportionality is a key guiding principle in the Bank’s approach to regulations. In deliberating regulatory policies and requirements, the Bank strives to strike a balance between achieving the desired policy objectives to safeguard public interest and minimising regulatory burden for the financial services industry. A risk-proportionate design in regulation takes into account various factors that affect the degree of potential risk, such as the complexity and scale of operations, the types of potential risks, as well as the maturity of industry players to appropriately manage risks. Accordingly, the Bank practices three gatekeeping regimes – license, approval and registration, where the level of regulatory requirements are differentiated. Activities requiring a license will generally attract stricter requirements, compared to activities requiring approval. Similarly, activities that require registration will attract less regulations compared to an approval.

How do I know if my business is regulated?​​ (A guidance sample)

  • The information in this section seeks to provide interested applicants with a general example on how the applicability of regulations may be determined. Applicants are responsible to comprehensively assess the potential legal implications of the services they intend to offer vis-à-vis regulations administered by the Bank or other regulators such as the Securities Commission. It is common practice to engage an appropriate legal consultant to conduct such an assessment where an applicant is not well positioned to do so. ​​
  • Applicants are reminded that a biased interpretation of the Bank’s regulations causing non-compliance may result in potential enforcement actions. ​ ​​
  • Applicants in need of guidance or broad advice concerning legal and compliance matters may also consider participating in MDEC’s Fintech Booster program. When in doubt, applicants may contact the Bank at fintech@bnm.gov.my​
Step 1: Determine applicable scope of regulated activity or entity​

Example:

An applicant intends to provide a mobile based e-wallet service for the purpose of facilitating retail transactions. The e-wallet seeks to enable the use of ringgit as a means of payment to merchants that are partnered with an existing registered merchant acquirer. The applicant has a total shareholders’ funds of RM4 million.​

What is the potential scope of regulated activity or entity that may be applicable?

Issuance of e-money

Step 2: Identify definition of regulated activity or entity under the legislation or guidelines​

Example:

Under Section 4 of the Guideline on the Electronic Money (e-Money).​

  • E-money is defined in the Payment Systems (Designated Payments Instruments) Order 2003 as a payment instrument, where tangible or intangible that:
    • stores funds electronically in exchange of funds paid to the insurer; and
    • is able to be used as a means of making payment to any person other than the issuer.
  • Issuer of e-money refers to any person that is responsible for the payment obligation and assumes the liabiliities for the e-money being issued.

Does the business model fit the definition of the regulated activity or entity?

If yes, proceed to next step

If no, reconsider if there are the other potential scope of regulated activity or entity that may be applicable

If unsure, seek appropriate legal consultation or guidance from Fintech Booster Program. If you are unable to obtain a clear answer, you may submit an inquiry to fintech@bnm.gov.my

Step 3: Read through the regulatory requirements and identify requirements that may be a challenge for you to comply with.​

Example:

Under Paragraph 14.2 of the Guideline on Electronic Money (e-Money).​

  • Large e-money issuers are required to maintain, at all times, minimum shareholders' funds unimpaired by losses of RM5million or 8% of their outstanding e-money liabilities, whichever is higher.

Does the proposed business model face a challenge in satisfying the requirement?

In this example, the applicant only has a total shareholders’ fund of RM4 million and faces significant difficulty in obtaining further funding. However, they expect to operate a large e-money scheme by extending the wallet limit beyond the RM200 limit for a small e-money scheme.​

Step 4: Provide a clear rationale how the regulatory impediment affects you’re the value proposition of your business model​

Example:

Scenario 1 – Applicant is able to satisfy requirement: ​​

The applicant manages to obtain additional funding of RM3 million and has no issues with the earlier impediment identified​

Scenario 2 – Applicant makes a change in business model: ​​

The applicant reconsiders the business model and decides it is feasible to start off as a small e-money scheme​ ​​

Scenario 3 – Applicant applies to the Regulatory Sandbox:​​​

The applicant did not manage to find a solution to comply with the regulatory requirements and submits an application to the Regulatory Sandbox​ ​​
