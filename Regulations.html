General Guidance on the Bank's Regulations

The principal role of the Bank is to promote monetary and financial stability that is conducive to the sustainable growth of the Malaysian economy.

In doing so, the Bank is entrusted with several functions including the fostering of a sound, progressive and inclusive financial system. Accordingly, the Bank’s regulations are based on robust principles and are designed to achieve parity, proportionality and neutrality (more about principles)​

Regulatory objectives – why do we regulate?​



AML/CFT

Prevent the abuse of financial services and illicit purposes

Effective anti-money laundering and anti-terrorism financing regimes are essential to protect the integrity of markets and of the broader financial system as they help mitigate the risk factors that contribute towards the abuse of financial services for ilicit purposes.

Key objectives of AML/CFT regulations:

  • Effective management of money laundering and/or terrorist financing risks through appropriate mitigation measures
  • Prevent the infiltration and abuse by criminals to the reporting institutions

Read more about AML/CFT regulations
Market conduct

Promote fair and responsible market practices

As part of the Bank’s principal regulatory objective to promote financial stability, the Bank strives to foster fair, responsible and professional business conduct of financial service providers and to protect the rights and interests of consumers of financial services and products.

Key objectives of market conduct regulations:

  • Promoting fair and equitable market practices
  • Ensure consumers are adequately protected
  • Foster financial literacy and capability of consumers

Read more about market conduct
Prudential

Ensure sound financial and business practices

A robust, effective and efficient prudential regulatory framework that is responsive to changing economic conditions is critical to maintain the resilience of financial institutions and the broader financial sector, while providing an environment conducive for financial innovation and growth.

Key objectives of prudential regulations:

  • Able to withstand adverse economic condition and maintain customer confidence
  • Address moral hazards by mitigating excessive risk-taking behaviour
  • Protection of depositors and policyholders by preventing mismanagement of funds

Key components of prudential regulation

  • Ensure sufficient liquidity and capital adequacy
  • Good corporate governance
  • Sound risk management and limits

Read more about prudential regulations


Technology risks

Cybersecurity, data privacy and operational resilience

The Bank is entrusted to ensure safety, soundness and robustness of financial service providers and the broader financial industry. An effective supervision framework is necessary to foster confidence of consumers and businesses in the financial sector. This includes supervision of technology-related risks, including cybersecurity, data privacy and operational risks.

Read more about supervision
Monetary stability

Maintain price stability in the Malaysian economy

The Bank relies on both financial regulations and monetary policy tools, to promote monetary stability, as well as safeguard and manage the value of the Ringgit in a manner that is conducive to the sustainable growth of the Malaysian economy. This includes the Foreign Exchange Policy which seeks to facilitate a conducive environment for cross-border real economic activities is the.

Read more about foreign exchange policy
Financial development

Foster a progressive and inclusive financial system

The Bank plays a crucial role in the development of a progressive and inclusive financial sector which entails preserving the core foundations of financial stability at all times, ensuring effective and efficient financial intermediation, and contributing towards economic growth and development.

Read more about financial sector development

Scope of regulated entities and activities

  • Proportionality is a key guiding principle in the Bank’s approach to regulations, which aims to strike a balance between achieving policy objectives to safeguard public interest and minimising regulatory burden for financial service providers.​​
  • A risk-proportionate design takes into account various factors that affect the degree of potential risk, such as the complexity and scale of operations, the types of potential risks, as well as the maturity of industry players to appropriately manage risks.​ ​​
  • Accordingly, the Bank practices three gatekeeping regimes – license, approval and registration, which have differentiated levels of regulatory requirements (in descending order from left to right).

How do I know if my business is regulated?​​ (A guidance sample)

  • The information in this section seeks to provide interested applicants with a general example on how the applicability of regulations may be determined. Applicants are responsible to comprehensively assess the potential legal implications of the services they intend to offer vis-à-vis regulations administered by the Bank or other regulators such as the Securities Commission. It is common practice to engage an appropriate legal consultant to conduct such an assessment where an applicant is not well positioned to do so. ​​
  • Applicants are reminded that a biased interpretation of the Bank’s regulations causing non-compliance may result in potential enforcement actions. ​ ​​
  • Applicants in need of guidance or broad advice concerning legal and compliance matters may also consider participating in MDEC’s Fintech Booster program . When in doubt, applicants may contact the Bank at fintech@bnm.gov.my​
Step 1: Determine applicable scope of regulated activity or entity​

E.g. Issuance of e-money

Step 2: Identify definition of regulated activity or entity under the legislation or guidelines​

E.g. E-money is defined in the Payment Systems (Designated Payment Instruments) Order 2003 as a payment instrument, whether tangible or intangible that:​

  1. stores funds electronically in exchange of funds paid to the issuer; and
  2. is able to be used as a means of making payment to any person other than the issuer.

Issuer of e-money refers to any person that is responsible for the payment obligation and assumes the liabilities for the e-money being issued.

Step 3: Read through the regulatory requirements and identify requirements that may be a challenge for you to comply with.​

E.g. section 13 prohibits issuer of e-money from issuing e-money at a discount, i.e. e-money that has a monetary value greater than the sum received.

Step 4: Provide a clear rationale how the regulatory impediment affects you’re the value proposition of your business model​

Example:

Scenario 1 – Applicant is able to satisfy requirement: ​​

The applicant manages to obtain additional funding of RM3 million and has no issues with the earlier impediment identified​

Scenario 2 – Applicant makes a change in business model: ​​

The applicant reconsiders the business model and decides it is feasible to start off as a small e-money scheme​ ​​

Scenario 3 – Applicant applies to the Regulatory Sandbox:​​​

The applicant did not manage to find a solution to comply with the regulatory requirements and submits an application to the Regulatory Sandbox​ ​​
